Issue 1 - Critical Missing Assumption: Guarantee of Essential Personnel Loyalty/Compliance Post-Purge

The plan relies heavily on immediately purging or replacing key professional staff (Decisions 5, 6, 14) while simultaneously assuming existing operational continuity (e.g., electricity, water, complex finance clearing). The missing assumption is the guaranteed ideological compliance or, alternatively, the success rate of quickly training replacements for specialized technical roles (e.g., fiber engineers, specialized medical technicians, complex legal interpreters) after the 'zero-tolerance' review. The current assumption relies on retaining 70% of non-policy staff, but assumes their operational function will continue unchanged, ignoring the potential for passive resistance or capacity degradation due to stress/fear.

Recommendation: Validate the 70% retention rate assumption by establishing a separate, verifiable 'Operational Proficiency Score' for retained staff in critical infrastructures (utilities, finance). If the average efficiency drops below 85% of pre-takeover levels, immediately activate the tertiary hiring track for vetted external contractors (as considered in Decision 5's minor strategies) to supplement critical deficiencies, even if it slightly compromises the 70% retention goal.

Sensitivity: If the actual efficiency of retained technical staff drops by 20% (baseline efficiency near 100%) due to ideological conflict or lack of motivation, critical system failure (Risk 2) becomes highly probable. This could trigger an estimated $400M-$750M in unforeseen remediation costs and delay ROI realization by 4-8 months due to sustained operational decay in services like banking or utility management.

Issue 2 - Unrealistic Assumption: Tacit Non-Interference from G7 Nations via Supply Guarantees

The plan assumes international political risk (Risk 1) can be mitigated by securing private, non-public confirmation of tacit non-interference from the G7 via energy and semiconductor guarantees (Assumption 4). Given the plan's highly aggressive and public nature ('Pioneer's Surge'), assuming major geopolitical rivals will silently accept immediate, unilateral governmental nullification in exchange for future supply guarantees is unrealistic. Such an agreement would likely require a public, verifiable trade-off that this plan cannot offer, as the core project goals contradict international norms on sovereignty.

Recommendation: Replace the assumption of 'tacit non-interference' with a detailed, funded 'Economic Sanctions Resilience Plan.' Assume a baseline 25% immediate global trade tariff/sanction impact post-nullification. Shift focus from preventing condemnation (Decision 8) to minimizing economic damage by diversifying primary transaction partners (e.g., expanding immediate trade focus towards non-G7 allies) and hardening domestic financial reserves against USD/EUR freezes.

Sensitivity: If the assumed G7 tacit agreement fails to materialize (0% effectiveness vs. assumed 50% mitigation), the project faces international financial sanctions which, based on pre-event economic modeling, could reduce projected initial ROI by 15-25% within the first fiscal year due to capital access restriction and trade penalties (Baseline ROI: 12%).

Issue 3 - Missing Assumption: Cybersecurity Vulnerability of Migrated Network Infrastructure

The plan aggressively pushes for an immediate system switchover to the Mainland internet infrastructure (Decision 2), assuming the migrated network will be inherently secure. The critical missing assumption is the guaranteed security posture and patch cadence of the new Mainland-controlled infrastructure being imposed. If this new, integrated network has latent vulnerabilities or is unprepared for novel cyber-attacks designed to exploit integration points, the entire information domain (Decision 2) and financial systems (Decision 10) could be compromised simultaneously, far exceeding the localized risk identified in Risk 2.

Recommendation: Mandate a 14-day 'Cyber Red Team' exercise specifically targeting the integration points between the legacy Taiwanese network choke-points and the new Mainland CA structure before the 7-day essential service cutover (Assumption 2). Budget 15% of the Decision 13 infrastructure budget solely for third-party penetration testing of the target network architecture, focusing on Denial of Service vectors exploiting inter-system protocol translation.

Sensitivity: A critical zero-day exploit found in the new CA's authentication protocols (discovered post-migration) could lead to a total administrative shutdown for 7-10 days. This would bypass the projected 48-72 hour crisis (Risk 2) and could lead to the irreversible compromise of the national financial ledger (Decision 10), potentially requiring a total system rollback costing 20-35% of the total project budget.
